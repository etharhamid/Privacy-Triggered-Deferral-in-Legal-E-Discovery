\title{Privacy-Triggered Deferral in Legal E-Discovery:\\
Risk-Aware Routing for PII Redaction}

% ── Abstract ────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
Automated PII redaction in legal e-discovery relies on NER models whose
confidence scores proxy deployment safety.
We show this proxy is insufficient: NER confidence clusters in a narrow
high-confidence band (std~$=0.033$), providing almost no signal about
document-level privacy risk.
We propose \textbf{Privacy-Triggered Deferral}, a dual-objective
Learning-to-Defer (L2D) policy that routes documents to human reviewers
when either NER confidence is low \emph{or} predicted privacy risk is
high.
Document-level sensitivity labels are generated by a local LLM
(Qwen-2.5 7B); a multi-task DistilBERT model is then trained with joint
NER and risk-regression heads, distilling the LLM judgment into a
deployable scorer.
Under an e-discovery cost model ($C_{\text{leak}}{=}\$50$, $C_h{=}\$5$),
our method reduces true leakage (sensitive documents auto-processed with
incomplete redaction) from $5.5\%$ to $0.4\%$ and sensitive-document
exposure from $22.7\%$ to $4.6\%$ at $\tau_c{=}0.85$ (mean over 5
seeds) at comparable cost ($\$4.48$ vs.\ $\$4.57$/doc).
At tighter thresholds, true leakage reaches zero.
These results suggest that routing policies should incorporate risk
signals beyond model confidence in high-stakes domains.
\end{abstract}

% ================================================================
\section{Introduction}
% ================================================================

In legal e-discovery, organizations must redact Personally Identifiable
Information (PII) from large document corpora before production to
opposing counsel.
Manual review costs \$5--\$50 per document~\cite{ediscovery_costs},
motivating AI-based NER pipelines.
Standard L2D and Selective Prediction
frameworks~\cite{geifman2017selective, mozannar2020consistent} route
documents to human reviewers only when model confidence is \emph{low},
assuming high confidence implies safe automation.

This assumption has a critical blind spot.
A model may be highly confident yet the document may contain
high-severity PII (SSNs, credit card numbers) or multiple co-occurring
identifier types that amplify re-identification risk if any token is
missed---a \emph{collateral privacy leak}.
Because PII entities have clear orthographic signatures, NER confidence
clusters in a narrow band (std~$=0.033$), providing almost no
discriminative signal between high-risk and low-risk documents.

Effective human-AI collaboration requires more than routing on
uncertainty alone~\cite{collins2024building}.
In practice, deployed AI systems use context-dependent guardrails that go
beyond confidence thresholds~\cite{jorgensen2025fabric}; our work
operationalises this insight into a mathematically precise routing
policy.
We propose \textbf{Privacy-Triggered Deferral}, a dual-objective L2D
policy with a second trigger based on predicted contextual privacy risk,
directly implementing the \emph{Conditionally Autonomous AI} oversight
level~\cite{jorgensen2025fabric} where capability does not imply
permission.

\paragraph{Related work.}
Standard L2D optimises a joint loss over model and expert
predictions~\cite{mozannar2020consistent}; selective prediction abstains
when confidence is low~\cite{geifman2017selective}; both assume
confidence is the sole routing signal.
Our work introduces a \emph{second}, context-aware routing axis
orthogonal to confidence and specific to the privacy domain.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate*}[label=\textbf{(\roman*)}]
  \item We identify \emph{confidence insufficiency}: the narrow NER
    confidence band renders confidence-only deferral a poor discriminator
    of document-level privacy risk.
  \item We propose a dual-objective routing policy
    $\text{Defer}(x) = \mathbf{1}[c(x) < \tau_c \;\lor\; r(x) > \tau_r]$
    that reduces true leakage by ${\sim}14{\times}$ relative to
    confidence-only deferral.
  \item We train a multi-task DistilBERT with joint NER and
    risk-regression heads, where sensitivity labels are distilled from a
    local LLM\@.
  \item We show this leakage reduction comes at comparable cost, making
    it practical for privacy-conscious deployment.
\end{enumerate*}

\section{Method}
% ================================================================

\paragraph{Dataset.}
We use the \texttt{ai4privacy/pii-masking-400k}
dataset~\cite{ai4privacy}, selecting 500 English-language synthetic
documents with character-level PII annotations across 17 entity types.
Documents are split 64\%/16\%/20\% per seed; all results are
mean~$\pm$~std over 5 seeds (${\sim}47$ of 100 test documents are
sensitive).

\paragraph{Sensitivity labels.}
\label{sec:sensitivity}
The dataset provides PII spans but no document-level sensitivity scores.
For each document, a Qwen-2.5 7B model~\cite{qwen2025} receives the
full text and PII types and rates contextual sensitivity from 0 to 1,
considering PII severity, surrounding topic sensitivity,
re-identification risk, and PII type diversity.
Documents with $s(x) \geq 0.5$ are labeled sensitive (240/500, 48\%).
The LLM is used only during data preparation; at inference time, only
the DistilBERT risk head is used (see Appendix~\ref{app:dataset} for
the score distribution).
Figure~\ref{fig:workflow} illustrates the two-stage pipeline.

\paragraph{Model architecture.}
A shared \texttt{distilbert-base-uncased} backbone (66M parameters)
feeds two heads:
a \textbf{NER head} (Linear$(768 \!\to\! 2)$) producing per-token
redact/keep logits with confidence $c(x)$ as the mean max-probability
over entity-predicted tokens, and
a \textbf{Risk head} (Linear$(768 \!\to\! 128) \!\to\! \text{ReLU}
\!\to\! \text{Dropout}(0.1) \!\to\! \text{Linear}(128 \!\to\! 1)
\!\to\! \text{Sigmoid}$) operating on \texttt{[CLS]} to produce
$r(x) \in [0,1]$.
Both are trained jointly:
$\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{NER}} + \mathcal{L}_{\text{Risk}}$
(cross-entropy + MSE).
Training: AdamW, lr$=2{\times}10^{-5}$, batch 16, 3 epochs.

\paragraph{Cost model.}
Let $c(x)$ be NER confidence and $r(x)$ the predicted risk.
The human reviewer is modeled as an oracle~\cite{mozannar2020consistent}.
Per-document cost is:
\begin{equation}
  \text{Cost}(x) = 
  \begin{cases}
    C_h & \text{if } d(x) = \text{defer} \\[4pt]
    \underbrace{\textstyle\sum_t \mathbf{1}[\hat{y}_t \!\neq\! y_t]\cdot C_{\text{err}}}_{\text{missed-token cost}}
    + \underbrace{\mathbf{1}[\text{sens.}]\cdot \mathbf{1}[\text{miss}>0]\cdot r(x)\cdot C_{\text{leak}}}_{\text{leakage cost (if redaction incomplete)}}
    & \text{if } d(x) = \text{automate}
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:cost}
\end{equation}
with $C_h{=}\$5$, $C_{\text{err}}{=}\$2$,
$C_{\text{leak}}{=}\$50$~\cite{ediscovery_costs}.

\paragraph{Routing policies.}
We compare three strategies of increasing oversight:

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(1)~Autonomous AI\,:}
Automate all documents;
$\text{Defer}(x) = \text{False}\ \forall x$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(2)~Confidence-based L2D\,:}
Defer when confidence is low (standard selective prediction):
\begin{equation}
  \text{Defer}(x) = \mathbf{1}[c(x) < \tau_c]
  \label{eq:conf}
\end{equation}

\noindent\textbf{(3)~Privacy-Triggered L2D} (proposed)\textbf{\,:}
Defer when confidence is low \emph{or} risk is high:
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\text{Defer}(x) = \mathbf{1}[c(x) < \tau_c \;\lor\; r(x) > \tau_r]}
  \label{eq:privacy}
\end{equation}
The OR condition routes high-confidence documents that are contextually
dangerous to a human reviewer---a capability Eq.~\ref{eq:conf}
structurally cannot provide.

\section{Experiments}
% ================================================================

\paragraph{Setup.}
We sweep $\tau_c \in \{0.75, \ldots, 0.95\}$,
$\tau_r \in \{0.2, \ldots, 0.6\}$, and
$C_h \in \{\$2, \ldots, \$40\}$ (full grids in
Appendix~\ref{app:sweeps}).
Key metrics: \emph{automation rate}, \emph{sensitive-document exposure}
(\% sensitive docs auto-processed), \emph{true leakage rate}
(\% sensitive docs auto-processed \emph{and} incompletely redacted),
\emph{system F1}, and \emph{expected cost} (Eq.~\ref{eq:cost}).
On a representative seed, NER confidence spans $[0.725, 0.900]$
(mean~$=0.826$, std~$=0.033$); sensitive and non-sensitive documents
have nearly identical mean confidence ($0.825$ vs.\ $0.827$),
confirming confidence alone cannot separate them.

\paragraph{Results.}
Table~\ref{tab:main} reports results at representative thresholds.
At $\tau_c{=}0.85$, confidence-only exposes $22.7\%$ of sensitive
documents, of which $5.5\%$ suffer incomplete redaction (true leakage).
Privacy-triggered at the same $\tau_c$ with $\tau_r{=}0.5$ cuts
exposure to $4.6\%$ and true leakage to $0.4\%$---a
${\sim}14{\times}$ reduction---at comparable cost ($\$4.48$ vs.\
$\$4.57$/doc).
At tighter thresholds ($\tau_c{=}0.75, \tau_r{=}0.4$), true leakage
reaches $0.0\%$.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
% ================================================================

The narrow NER confidence distribution (std~$=0.033$) is a structural
property of well-annotated PII data, where entity boundaries are
orthographically unambiguous.
Confidence-only deferral cannot distinguish sensitive from non-sensitive
documents (mean confidence: $0.825$ vs.\ $0.827$); the risk head
captures what confidence cannot.
Privacy-Triggered Deferral benefits documents in the high-confidence,
high-risk region---e.g., documents containing SSNs alongside other
identifiers, where entity detection is trivial but combined context
amplifies exposure if any token is missed (see Appendix~\ref{app:analysis}
for detailed analysis including failure cases).

The fundamental trade-off is \emph{true leakage vs.\ automation rate}
under asymmetric penalties.
The $\tau_r{=}0.4$ operating point provides a conservative zero-leakage
default; raising to $\tau_r{=}0.5$ increases automation to $13\%$ but
introduces $0.4\%$ true leakage on average
(Appendix~\ref{app:sensitivity}).
The autonomous baseline risks \emph{automation bias}; confidence-only at
$\tau_c{=}0.95$ exhibits \emph{under-reliance}; Privacy-Triggered
Deferral targets a middle ground.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Sensitivity labels are generated by a 7B-parameter LLM, not human
experts; low true leakage reflects successful approximation of LLM
judgment, not elimination of contextual privacy risk.
The evaluation uses 500 synthetic documents with 100-document test sets;
validation on real legal corpora is needed.
The oracle human assumption (100\% accuracy) overestimates real reviewer
performance; a noisy-human model would shift optimal thresholds.

\paragraph{Conclusion.}
We demonstrated that model confidence is an insufficient routing proxy
in legal e-discovery.
Privacy-Triggered Deferral reduces true leakage by ${\sim}14{\times}$
relative to confidence-only deferral ($0.4\%$ vs.\ $5.5\%$, mean over 5
seeds) at comparable cost, while cutting sensitive-document exposure from
$22.7\%$ to $4.6\%$.
The core insight is that \emph{capability does not imply permission}:
routing policy design should encode risk signals beyond model confidence
in high-stakes domains.

\section{Dataset Distribution}
\label{app:dataset}

Figure~\ref{fig:dataset} visualises the LLM-annotated sensitivity score
distribution and PII type frequencies.
Of 500 documents, 240 (48\%) are labeled sensitive ($s(x){\geq}0.5$)
and 260 (52\%) non-sensitive.
High-risk PII types (SOCIALNUM, CREDITCARDNUMBER, IDCARDNUM) contribute
to higher sensitivity scores despite lower raw frequency than
GIVENNAME/SURNAME\@.
Because NER confidence is blind to PII \emph{type}---a name and an SSN
look equally unambiguous to the NER head---the confidence axis alone
cannot distinguish the two clusters.

\section{Detailed Analysis}
\label{app:analysis}

\paragraph{Why confidence alone fails.}
The confidence distribution (std~$=0.033$, min~$=0.725$) means all
documents appear safe by confidence alone---a structural property of
well-annotated PII data where entity boundaries are orthographically
unambiguous.
The confidence-only policy draws a vertical boundary in $(c(x), r(x))$
space (Fig.~\ref{fig:scatter}), but sensitive and non-sensitive
documents are distributed nearly uniformly across the confidence range
(mean: $0.825$ vs.\ $0.827$), making them indistinguishable.
The risk head captures what confidence cannot: an estimate of
document-level privacy severity.

\paragraph{When the risk trigger helps most.}
Privacy-Triggered Deferral benefits documents in the high-confidence,
high-risk region---e.g., a document containing an SSN alongside other
identifier types, where entity detection is trivial but combined context
amplifies exposure if any token is missed.

\paragraph{Failure cases and variance.}
With 320 training examples, the risk head's discrimination
(gap $=0.047$) is modest, and results show meaningful variance
(e.g., exposure $4.6\%{\pm}4.3\%$, true leakage $0.4\%{\pm}0.9\%$ at
$\tau_c{=}0.85, \tau_r{=}0.5$).
On most seeds the risk trigger achieves zero true leakage; on
occasional seeds a single sensitive document slips through.
The model tends toward over-flagging rather than under-flagging---the
correct failure mode for a safety-critical system.

\paragraph{Routing trade-offs: over- and under-reliance.}
The autonomous baseline risks \emph{automation bias}: legal teams may
assume the AI handles sensitivity and omit audits.
Confidence-only at $\tau_c{=}0.95$ exhibits \emph{under-reliance}:
sending every document to human review defeats the system's purpose.
Privacy-Triggered Deferral targets a middle ground.
The oracle human (100\% accuracy) overestimates real reviewer
performance; future work should replace it with a noisy-human model.

\paragraph{Transparency and calibration.}
The routing decision (Eq.~\ref{eq:privacy}) is interpretable: a deferred
document carries an explanation---either model uncertainty or high
sensitivity classification.
Future work should apply temperature scaling to $r(x)$ so that $\tau_r$
maps to an explicit false-negative rate guarantee.

\subsection{Risk Threshold Sensitivity}
\label{app:sensitivity}

Table~\ref{tab:risk_sweep} isolates the sensitivity of $\tau_r$ at fixed
$\tau_c{=}0.85$.  Figure~\ref{fig:sweep} sweeps $\tau_r$,
showing $\tau_r{=}0.4$ as the cost-optimal zero-leakage threshold
(\$4.91/doc, 2\% automation, 0.0\% true leakage).
Raising $\tau_r$ to 0.5 increases automation to 13\% but introduces
$4.6\%$ exposure and $0.4\%$ true leakage on average; further raising to
0.6 brings $21.4\%$ exposure, $5.5\%$ true leakage, and \emph{increases}
total cost to \$4.60 due to leakage penalties.
This non-monotonic cost curve illustrates why naive automation-rate
maximisation is the wrong objective in privacy-critical settings.
